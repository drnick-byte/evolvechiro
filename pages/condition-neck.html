The Problem

Why your neck keeps hurting

The cervical spine is seven vertebrae stacked on top of each other, supporting a head that weighs 10–12 pounds. Every inch your head sits forward of neutral — which is the default posture for anyone who spends time at a desk or on a phone — adds roughly 10 additional pounds of effective load on the neck. By the time your head is 3 inches forward, your cervical spine is managing the equivalent of 40+ pounds.

That kind of sustained mechanical stress doesn't fix itself with rest or stretching. It creates joint restriction, disc pressure, muscle guarding, and nerve irritation that compounds over time. The result is the neck pain, stiffness, and headaches that most people have just learned to live with.

At Pinnacle Chiropractic, we do a full cervical evaluation — including posture analysis, range of motion testing, orthopedic and neurological screening, and digital X-rays when indicated — to identify exactly what's driving your symptoms. Then we correct it.

Headaches

When the pain starts in your neck, not your head

Cervicogenic headaches — headaches that originate from the cervical spine — are one of the most misdiagnosed and undertreated conditions in primary care. They're frequently labeled as tension headaches or migraines and managed with medication that addresses the symptom while the structural cause goes untreated.

The upper cervical spine (C1–C3) directly refers pain to the base of the skull, behind the eyes, and across the top of the head. If your headaches tend to start at the back of the neck and spread forward, worsen with certain neck positions, or come with stiffness and restricted range of motion — the cervical spine is likely the source.

Chiropractic adjustments targeting the upper cervical and cervicothoracic junction have strong clinical evidence for reducing both the frequency and intensity of cervicogenic headaches. For many patients, it's the first thing that's actually worked.

Signs your headaches may be cervicogenic:

  • Headache that starts at the base of the skull or neck
  • Pain that radiates from the neck to the forehead or behind one eye
  • Stiffness or restricted neck movement alongside the headache
  • Headaches triggered by neck positions or sustained postures
  • Tenderness at the upper neck on the same side as the headache
  • Pain that doesn't respond well to typical headache medications

Treatment

What your care plan looks like

Every plan is tailored to your specific findings. Most neck pain and headache cases at Pinnacle involve some combination of the following:

01

Cervical evaluation

Full range of motion assessment, posture analysis, orthopedic and neurological testing, and digital X-rays when clinically appropriate. We measure forward head translation, cervical curve, and segmental mobility to build a precise picture.

02

Cervical chiropractic adjustment

Specific manipulation to restore joint mobility, reduce nerve irritation, and decompress restricted cervical segments. Applied with precision to the levels identified in your evaluation — not a generalized neck crack.

03

Soft tissue therapy

Targeted work on the suboccipital muscles, scalenes, levator scapulae, and upper trapezius — the primary muscles involved in neck pain and tension headaches. Releases trigger points and restores normal muscle tone.

04

Postural correction

Practical, specific guidance on workstation setup, phone posture, sleeping positions, and movement habits — the things that created the problem and will recreate it without intervention.

05

Corrective exercise

A targeted home program focused on deep cervical flexor strengthening, scapular stabilization, and thoracic mobility — the foundations of a resilient cervical spine that holds its corrections.
