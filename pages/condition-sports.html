The Difference

What CCSP certification actually means

The Certified Chiropractic Sports Physician (CCSP) credential is a post-doctoral board certification through the American Chiropractic Board of Sports Physicians. It requires additional training in sports medicine, orthopedic evaluation, rehabilitation, and emergency procedures — on top of the standard chiropractic doctorate.

It means your provider has been specifically trained to evaluate and manage sports injuries using the same functional and biomechanical framework as a sports medicine physician. It's not just chiropractic applied to athletes — it's a different clinical lens.

For athletes and active patients, that distinction matters. The goal isn't just resolving the acute injury — it's identifying the movement patterns, structural imbalances, and biomechanical deficits that caused the injury and will cause the next one if left unaddressed.

The Approach

Treat the injury. Fix the movement.

Most sports injuries don't happen in isolation. A hamstring strain is often downstream from hip or lumbar dysfunction. Shoulder impingement is usually tied to thoracic mobility and scapular control. Ankle sprains are frequently connected to movement compensation patterns that have been building for months.

A CCSP-trained provider evaluates the whole kinetic chain — not just the symptomatic joint. That means the treatment plan addresses the root cause of the injury, not just the site of pain. It also means the rehabilitation protocol is designed to restore full athletic function, not just basic activity tolerance.

We work with recreational athletes, competitive athletes, and high school and college-level competitors. We also provide on-site coverage for events and competitions through Macomb County.

Signs you should see a sports chiropractor:

  • Pain or dysfunction that limits training or competition
  • An injury that "healed" but performance hasn't returned to baseline
  • Recurring injuries at the same site or in the same pattern
  • Joint pain, clicking, or instability during sport-specific movement
  • Post-competition soreness that's taking longer to resolve
  • You've been cleared by a doctor but still don't feel right

Treatment

What your care plan looks like

Sports injury management at Pinnacle is structured around return-to-sport timelines and functional goals — not just symptom relief. Every plan is built on your specific findings, sport demands, and performance objectives.

01

Comprehensive sports evaluation

Orthopedic testing, functional movement screening, and sport-specific assessment. We evaluate the injury in the context of your whole kinetic chain — not just the painful structure — to understand why it happened.

02

Chiropractic adjustment

Targeted spinal and extremity manipulation to restore joint mobility, reduce protective muscle guarding, and improve neural communication to the affected area. Extremity adjusting — shoulder, knee, ankle, wrist — is a core part of sports chiropractic that most general practitioners don't perform.

03

Soft tissue therapy

Active release, instrument-assisted, and manual techniques targeting the specific muscles, tendons, and fascia involved in the injury. Applied to both the injured structure and the compensating tissues that are now under abnormal load.

04

Sport-specific rehabilitation

Progressive exercise programming that moves from pain relief to tissue loading to sport-specific movement patterns. Designed around your actual sport demands and competitive timeline — not a generic rehab protocol.

05

Return-to-sport clearance

Functional testing and sport-specific movement assessment before full return to competition. The goal is full performance — not just getting you back on the field before you're ready to perform at your level.

What to Expect

Your first visit

Plan for 60 minutes on your first appointment. We'll take a detailed injury history, perform a thorough orthopedic and functional evaluation, and discuss findings and a care plan with you directly. If appropriate, we'll begin treatment that day.

Acute sports injuries often resolve in 4–8 visits when addressed early and correctly. Chronic injuries or those with significant tissue damage take longer. We'll give you a realistic timeline based on your specific findings — not a vague "it depends."
